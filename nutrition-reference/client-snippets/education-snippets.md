# Client Education Snippets

Reusable blurbs — drop these into nutrition guides, emails, or say them almost verbatim in sessions. Written in plain, non-judgmental language matching the "teach, don't prescribe" voice.

---

### Why protein matters
Protein is what keeps muscle on your frame while you're losing fat, and what builds it while you're gaining. It also keeps you fuller for longer than carbs or fat per calorie — so hitting your protein target is usually the single highest-leverage thing you can do for both results and hunger control.

### Why fiber matters
Fiber (from vegetables, fruit, beans, and whole grains) slows digestion, helps you feel full on fewer calories, and supports gut health. It's also the easiest macro to under-eat without realizing it — most people fall short of 25-35g/day without paying attention.

### "Healthy" isn't the same as "fits your goal"
A food can be nutritious and still not fit what you're working toward right now, and a food can be less "clean" and still fit perfectly fine. Avocado toast and a candy bar can have similar calories — neither is "bad," they just play different roles. The goal is understanding what a food *does* in the context of your day, not sorting foods into good/bad.

### Why we don't do rigid meal plans
A meal plan tells you what to eat. Coaching teaches you how to build your own meals so the skill stays with you after we stop working together. The destination — understanding protein/carbs/fat well enough to build your own plate anywhere, anytime — is the actual goal.

### Eating out doesn't have to break your day
Most restaurant meals can fit your goals with small adjustments: protein-forward order, sauce on the side, swap fries for a side salad or veggies. The skill is estimating roughly what's on your plate, not avoiding restaurants forever.

### What to do if you're hungry between meals
First check: did you hit your protein and fiber targets today? Low-calorie, high-volume foods (vegetables, fruit, popcorn, broth-based soups) are the best lever for fullness without blowing through your numbers.

### On alcohol
Alcohol has calories but no protein, carbs, or fat that "count" toward those macros — it just adds to total calories. Occasional drinks fit fine in a flexible approach; the only real coaching note is that more frequent or large quantities, the swap is to budget calories elsewhere that day.

### On tracking (MyFitnessPal or similar)
Tracking isn't forever — it's a tool to build awareness of portion sizes and what's actually in your food. Most people need a few weeks of consistent tracking before they can eyeball portions accurately on their own.
